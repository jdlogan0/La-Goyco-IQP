Introduction

Noise pollution, defined as the intrusion of unwarranted sounds, is associated with adverse health effects such as cardiovascular problems, mental health impacts, hearing loss, and cognitive impairment (Kloth et al., 2019). High noise levels increase the risk of individuals experiencing hearing impairment by 30%, and 13.5% of deaths are connected to high blood pressure, a direct consequence of noise pollution (Cruickshanks et al., 2015; Europe, 2011). The issue of noise pollution is significant in Puerto Rico, where more than 21% experience hearing loss in at least one ear (Cruickshanks et al., 2015). Among U.S. jurisdictions, Puerto Rico has the highest percentage of its population exposed to noise pollution (dB levels exceeding 70) at 44%, surpassing the average of 30% (Neitzel et al., 2023). This handbook aims to give a background on noise pollution in Puerto Rico and how to make improvements to help minimize noise.

Sources of Noise

Vehicles

Vehicles in Puerto Rico contribute to levels of noise pollution through the modification of the exhaust pipe and muffler. Cars are specifically adjusted to create more noise. Along with the modifications of cars, the use of car horns causes short bursts of high levels of noise.

Voceteo

In a subsection to vehicular noise, voceteo in San Juan has created an unnecessarily high level of noise. In January of 2023, the Senate of Puerto Rico made amendments to the Vehicle and Traffic Law to prevent the use of vehicles on public roads or land that generate excessive noise or surpass the sound pressure limits set by legislation. There are fines set in place, the basic fine would be $500, with an additional fine of $250 if this occurs in a school zone or anytime between 10pm and 6am.

Business

In Puerto Rico, the increase in tourism activities such as the number of restaurants, bars, hotels, and travel caused elevated noise levels, amplifying the potential risks to the well-being of its residents (Backiel et al., 2004). Bars and clubs are suspected to play music at high levels to attract more customers to their establishments.

Personal Noise Reduction

Outside of policy changes, there are ways inside the household to combat noise. One option that was found is the addition of carpeting in a household. Carpets and the padding under them absorb sound waves to conceal the reverberation of noise. Curtains as well are a great tool to combat exterior noise. The density and thickness of the curtain material impede the transmission of sound from the outside. Furniture plays a role in dampening the effects of noise. Plush furniture, cushions and upholstery absorb sound and contribute to a quieter environment. Door draft stoppers block the entry of external noise from entering a room. Rubber stoppers and pads on the bottom of table and chair legs reduces the amount of vibrations transmitted through the floor of a room. Indoor plants and foliage can help absorb high-frequency and provide a natural and aesthetically pleasing way to enhance the acoustic environment. The effectiveness of each item may vary, and combining multiple strategies can provide a more comprehensive noise reduction in a given space.

Policy

Puerto Rico's current regulations addressing noise pollution are described in the Public Order Code. Article 2,201 within the Public Order Code outlines the legal framework associated with noise pollution, emphasizing the importance of refraining from emitting or distributing noise that exceeds specified decibel level limits, contingent on the time of day. According to the law, the permissible sound levels are set at 65 decibels between 7 am and 10 pm and lowered to 55 decibels during nighttime hours. Noise Mapping

Noise mapping is a valuable tool to assess and visualize noise levels across a given region, pinpointing areas of concern, and helping decision-makers understand the sources and levels of noise a population is exposed to. This information can be used to guide governments in forming noise reduction plans that are most effective for the area being mapped (Liu et al., 2020; Marques & Pitarma, 2019; Murphy & King, 2014).
